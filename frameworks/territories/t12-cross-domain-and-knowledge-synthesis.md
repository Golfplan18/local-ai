# Framework — Cross-Domain and Knowledge Synthesis

*Self-contained framework for synthesizing across domains, integrating disparate knowledge bodies, or holding thesis/antithesis in productive tension. Compiled 2026-05-01.*

---

## Territory Header

- **Territory ID:** T12
- **Name:** Cross-Domain and Knowledge Synthesis
- **Super-cluster:** E (Synthesis, Orientation, Structure, Generation)
- **Characterization:** Operations that synthesize across domains, integrate disparate knowledge bodies, or hold thesis and antithesis in productive tension.
- **Boundary conditions:** Input is two-or-more domains, knowledge bodies, or positions to be integrated. Excludes within-domain analysis. Excludes paradigm-comparison (T9 — examining frames rather than integrating across them).
- **Primary axis:** Stance (integrative vs. thesis-antithesis vs. cross-domain analogical).
- **Secondary axes:** Specificity (general integration vs. cross-domain analogical).
- **Coverage status:** Strong after existing population (Cross-Domain Analogical deferred per CR-6).

---

## When to use this framework

Use T12 when the user has two or more developed positions, frameworks, knowledge bodies, or domains and wants them brought into productive relation — either by integration into a unified picture, or by holding them in dialectical tension that produces something new. T12 answers questions like "how do these two frameworks connect?", "what's the structural parallel between X and Y?", "drive thesis through antithesis to synthesis", and "what new understanding emerges when we hold these in tension?".

T12 does NOT do paradigm comparison without integration (that is T9 frame-comparison), choice-among-options (T3), strongest-case-construction for one position (T15 steelman), or generative open exploration (T20).

## Within-territory disambiguation

```
[Territory identified: cross-domain or knowledge synthesis]

Q1 (stance): "Are you trying to integrate two or more bodies of knowledge into
              a unified picture, or hold a thesis and an antithesis in productive
              tension to see what new understanding emerges?"
  ├─ "integrate into unified picture" → synthesis (Tier-2)
  ├─ "thesis and antithesis in tension" → dialectical-analysis (Tier-2)
  ├─ "find a structural analogy across very different domains" →
        cross-domain-analogical (deferred per CR-6)
  └─ ambiguous → synthesis with escalation hook to dialectical-analysis
```

**Default route.** `synthesis` at Tier-2 when ambiguous (the integrative baseline; dialectical is the productive-tension variant).

**Escalation hooks.**
- After `synthesis` Tier-2: if irreducible tensions remain that resist integration, hook sideways to `dialectical-analysis` (the productive-tension variant is appropriate when integration is forced).
- After `dialectical-analysis`: if a synthesis emerges from the tension and can be articulated, loop back to `synthesis` for the unified output.
- After either T12 mode: if the analysis is really about which paradigm to adopt rather than how to integrate them, hook sideways to T9 (`frame-comparison` or `worldview-cartography`).
- After either T12 mode: if the work is generative rather than integrative, hook sideways to T20 (`passion-exploration`).

## Mode entries

### `synthesis` — Synthesis

**Educational name:** cross-domain integrative synthesis (stance-integrative).

**Plain-language description.** Takes two-or-more frameworks or knowledge bodies as input and produces an integrative synthesis: both frameworks named as peer roots (neither reduced to a special case of the other), at least one cross-link bridging them with mechanism-level evidence (not just shared vocabulary), productive tensions surfaced explicitly rather than smoothed over, an emergent insight that no single framework would produce alone, and limitations where the synthesis breaks down. The mode is neutral examination, not advocacy.

**Critical questions.**
- CQ1: Are the proposed connections structural correspondences at the mechanism level, or surface analogies?
- CQ2: Do both frameworks survive the synthesis as peer roots, or has one been reduced to a special case of the other?
- CQ3: Have productive tensions been named explicitly, or smoothed over to produce apparent harmony?
- CQ4: Does the synthesis produce an emergent insight unavailable from either framework alone?

**Per-pipeline-stage guidance.**
- **Analyst.** Name both frameworks as peer roots; identify structural parallels; produce mechanism-level evidence per cross-link; surface productive tensions; identify emergent insight; name limitations.
- **Evaluator.** Apply the mechanism test to each cross-link; verify peer-root preservation; verify tensions named not smoothed; verify emergent insight identified.
- **Reviser.** Add mechanism evidence where vocabulary alone supported the cross-link; surface tensions where smoothed; restore peer-root status if one framework was reduced; resist apparent-harmony polish.
- **Verifier.** Confirm six required sections (frameworks_identified, structural_parallels, evidence_for_genuineness, emergent_insight, productive_tensions, limitations).
- **Consolidator.** Merge as a structured synthesis; matrix-friendly when parallels are tabular; prose-with-headings otherwise.

**Source tradition.** Cross-domain analogical mapping (Gentner-Forbus structure-mapping); structural-isomorphism detection.

**Lens dependencies.**
- Required: none.
- Optional: cross-domain-analogical-mapping (when frameworks come from distant domains), structural-isomorphism-detection.
- Foundational: kahneman-tversky-bias-catalog.

**Composition.** Atomic.

### `dialectical-analysis` — Dialectical Analysis

**Educational name:** thesis-antithesis dialectical analysis (stance-thesis-antithesis).

**Plain-language description.** Takes a position with sensed tension or apparent contradiction and drives it through adversarial commitment to both sides. The thesis is stated with claims to completeness; internal contradictions are surfaced; the antithesis is developed with adversarial commitment, emerging from those contradictions (not as external critique); a sublation either transcends the contradiction by mechanism (cancelling what was false in each position while preserving what was true) or honors the irreducibility (Adornian escape valve) when no genuine sublation is available. Recursion — the next-level contradictions the sublation generates — is named.

**Critical questions.**
- CQ1: Does the antithesis emerge from the thesis's own internal contradictions, or is it external critique?
- CQ2: Does the sublation transcend by mechanism, or does it average the two positions?
- CQ3: If no genuine sublation is available, has the analysis honored the irreducibility (Adornian escape valve) rather than forcing one?
- CQ4: Have the next-level contradictions the sublation generates been named explicitly?

**Per-pipeline-stage guidance.**
- **Analyst.** State the thesis with completeness claims; surface internal contradictions; develop antithesis with adversarial commitment from those contradictions; produce sublation with transcending mechanism OR declare irreducibility; name recursion.
- **Evaluator.** Verify antithesis emerges from thesis's contradictions (not external critique); verify sublation transcends rather than averages; verify Adornian escape valve invoked when sublation is forced; verify recursion named.
- **Reviser.** Strengthen antithesis where it reads as token; replace averaging language with transcending language; honor irreducibility where forced sublation appears; resist thesis-favorable framing.
- **Verifier.** Confirm seven required sections (or six with irreducibility-declaration replacing sublation): generating_question, thesis, internal_contradictions, antithesis, genuine_contradiction_or_irreducibility, sublation_or_irreducibility_declaration, recursion_acknowledgement.
- **Consolidator.** Merge as a structured synthesis; IBIS-friendly when dialectical structure renders as question/idea/pro/con.

**Source tradition.** Hegelian dialectic (Aufheben — cancel-and-preserve mechanism); Adornian negative dialectics (irreducibility); Marxist historical materialism (when material conditions structure the tension).

**Lens dependencies.**
- Required: hegelian-dialectic-aufheben.
- Optional: adornian-negative-dialectics (when irreducibility is in play), marxist-historical-materialism (when material conditions structure the tension).
- Foundational: kahneman-tversky-bias-catalog.

**Composition.** Atomic.

## Lens references (Core Structure embedded)

### Hegelian Dialectic — Aufheben (required for dialectical-analysis)

**Core Structure.** The dialectical movement comprises three moments:

1. **Thesis.** A position stated with claims to completeness (the position takes itself as adequate to its object).
2. **Antithesis.** The position's own internal contradictions, when developed, produce an opposing position. Crucially, the antithesis is not external critique imposed from outside; it emerges from the thesis's incompleteness when the thesis is taken seriously and pressed.
3. **Sublation (Aufheben).** A higher position that *cancels* what was false in both thesis and antithesis while *preserving* what was true. The German verb "aufheben" carries this dual meaning: to cancel/lift-up/preserve simultaneously. A sublation is not a compromise (averaging the two) and not a third position alongside the first two; it is a transcendence that incorporates and supersedes.

The mechanism test for genuine sublation: name what was false in the thesis (cancelled), what was false in the antithesis (cancelled), what was true in the thesis (preserved), what was true in the antithesis (preserved), and how the sublation accomplishes both moves at once. If the proposed sublation cannot pass this test, the analysis is averaging, not sublating.

A sublation is also recursive: every sublation becomes a new thesis whose own contradictions, when developed, produce a new antithesis. Naming the next-level contradictions is part of honest dialectical analysis.

### Adornian Negative Dialectics (optional for dialectical-analysis)

**Core Structure.** Adorno's modification of Hegel: not every contradiction admits genuine sublation. Some oppositions are *constitutively irreducible* — they cannot be transcended without falsifying one or both terms. Forcing a sublation in these cases produces what Adorno calls "false reconciliation" — an apparent resolution that papers over the actual incompatibility.

The Adornian escape valve, when invoked, requires the analysis to:
1. State explicitly that no genuine sublation is available.
2. Name the structural reason (e.g., the two positions answer different questions; their irreducibility is constitutive of the domain).
3. Decline to produce a forced third term.

Adornian analysis treats honest standoff as a finding, not a failure. It guards against the Hegelian temptation to manufacture sublation when the contradiction is genuine.

### Cross-Domain Analogical Mapping (optional for synthesis)

**Core Structure.** Drawn from Gentner's structure-mapping theory and Forbus's later work, cross-domain analogical mapping treats analogies as *structural correspondences* rather than *surface similarities*. A genuine analogy maps:
- **Higher-order relations** between domain A and domain B (causal patterns, dependency structures, generative mechanisms).
- Not **attribute matches** (A and B share features) which produce surface analogy.

The mechanism test: a structural analogy can be falsified by examining a case where one domain's mechanism operates and the other's does not. A surface analogy collapses under the test (the "shared theme" turns out to be a verbal coincidence). The structure-mapping discipline keeps cross-domain syntheses honest by forcing each cross-link through the mechanism test.

### Structural-Isomorphism Detection (optional for synthesis)

**Core Structure.** Two frameworks are structurally isomorphic when their relational topologies map onto each other element-for-element, with identity of relation preserved. Genuine isomorphism survives transformations (relabeling elements does not break the correspondence); pseudo-isomorphism breaks under transformation. The detection discipline: state the mapping function explicitly (which element of A corresponds to which element of B) and test for stability under perturbation.
