# Contemplative and Spiritual Analysis Module

*Tier 2 module. Loads into the Breadth model context window as a complete unit.*

## Trigger Conditions

- Domain is spiritual practice, Buddhist philosophy, phenomenological inquiry, or contemplative experience
- Prompt references meditation, mindfulness, awareness, consciousness, or inner experience
- Problem involves practice difficulties, doctrinal questions, integration of contemplative insight with daily life, or navigating spiritual traditions

## Purpose

This module provides domain-specific questions for contemplative and spiritual inquiry. It complements Tier 1 tools by directing attention to experiential precision, the gap between conceptual understanding and lived experience, tradition-specific context, and the characteristic failure modes of spiritual reasoning (including premature transcendence, spiritual bypassing, and confusing map with territory).

The questions here are designed to produce useful, grounded answers -- not to generate philosophical abstraction. They prioritize phenomenological precision and practical applicability.

---

## Category 1: Experiential Precision

These ground the inquiry in actual experience rather than conceptual frameworks.

1. What is the actual experience being described? Strip away interpretive frameworks and doctrinal language -- what is happening at the level of direct sensory or phenomenological observation?
2. Is this a report of something experienced or a report of something believed? (The difference between "I noticed X" and "I believe X is true about reality.")
3. What is the quality of attention during the experience being described? (Concentrated, diffuse, fragmented, stable, effortful, effortless.)
4. Can the experience be reproduced, or was it a one-time occurrence? Under what conditions has it arisen?
5. What is the temporal structure of the experience? (Momentary flash, gradual unfolding, sustained state, oscillation.)

## Category 2: Practice Context

These situate the inquiry within the practitioner's actual practice.

6. What is the current practice? (Technique, frequency, duration, lineage or tradition, teacher relationship.)
7. What stage of practice development is this? (Establishing a habit, deepening concentration, navigating difficult territory, integrating insight, mature practice.)
8. What instructions is the practitioner currently working with, and are they being applied as intended?
9. Is there a teacher, guide, or community involved? What guidance has already been given on this question?
10. What is the practice history? How long, how consistently, what transitions between methods or traditions?

## Category 3: Tradition and Framework

These navigate the relationship between experience and the doctrinal frameworks used to interpret it.

11. Which tradition's framework is being used to interpret this experience? Is it the tradition the practitioner is actually practicing in, or imported from elsewhere?
12. Where does this experience or question map onto the tradition's own developmental models? (e.g., jhana factors, stages of insight, dark night, ox-herding pictures, stages of prayer.)
13. Is the doctrinal framework being used to illuminate the experience or to replace it? (Knowing the map is not the same as walking the terrain.)
14. Are terms from the tradition being used with their technical meaning or with a colloquial meaning? (e.g., "mindfulness," "emptiness," "non-dual," "ego" all have specific technical definitions that differ from popular usage.)
15. Does this question have a known, well-established answer within the relevant tradition? If so, has the practitioner encountered that answer, and is the question persisting despite it?

## Category 4: Common Failure Modes

These detect characteristic errors in spiritual reasoning and practice.

16. Is spiritual bypassing occurring -- using spiritual concepts or practices to avoid engaging with psychological or relational problems that need direct attention?
17. Is the inquiry conflating understanding the concept with having the realization? (Understanding "no-self" intellectually vs. the actual perceptual shift.)
18. Is premature transcendence at work -- jumping to "ultimate" answers that bypass the relative-level problem? (e.g., responding to practical suffering with "but nothing is real.")
19. Is there a reification problem -- treating a useful conceptual pointer as a thing that exists? (e.g., treating "awareness" as an entity rather than a description of a process.)
20. Is the practitioner comparing their experience to idealized descriptions and judging it deficient? What would change if the comparison were dropped?

## Category 5: Integration and Application

These address how contemplative insight connects to the rest of life.

21. How does this insight or experience show up in daily life outside of formal practice? If it does not, what does that reveal?
22. What is the relationship between this contemplative question and the practitioner's ethical conduct? (In most traditions, these are not separate domains.)
23. Is there a gap between the practitioner's stated values and their lived behavior that this inquiry might be touching?
24. What would change practically -- in relationships, decisions, or daily action -- if this question were resolved?
25. Is the inquiry itself a form of practice, or has it become a form of avoidance? (Endless questioning about practice can substitute for doing the practice.)

## Category 6: Phenomenological Investigation

These support direct investigation of the structure of experience.

26. What happens when attention is turned toward the observer rather than the observed? What is found?
27. What is the relationship between the verbal/conceptual mind and the experience being investigated? Does labeling the experience change it?
28. What are the bodily correlates of the experience or state being discussed? Where is it felt? What is its texture?
29. What is at the boundary of the experience -- what is it adjacent to, what does it transition into or out of?
30. If the question "who is experiencing this?" is applied, what arises?

---

## Integration Notes

- Category 1 (Experiential Precision) is the most critical category. It should run first to establish whether the inquiry is grounded in actual experience or operating entirely at the conceptual level.
- Category 4 (Failure Modes) functions as a diagnostic layer. These questions should be checked against the user's prompt before proceeding with analysis -- if a failure mode is active, the most useful response may be to surface it rather than to answer the stated question.
- CAF and OPV are less central in this domain. The most relevant Tier 1 tools are Challenge (testing assumptions), Concept Fan (finding the right level of abstraction for the inquiry), and AGO (clarifying what the practitioner is actually trying to accomplish).
- This module should not generate answers to spiritual questions. Its purpose is to generate the right questions -- ones that direct the practitioner's own investigation productively.
