# Political and Social Analysis Module

*Tier 2 module. Loads into the Breadth model context window as a complete unit.*

## Trigger Conditions

- Domain is political, social, or policy-related
- OPV reveals institutional correlation (stakeholder positions align with institutional interests)
- Prompt references governments, organizations, regulatory bodies, or advocacy groups

## Purpose

This module provides domain-specific questions for political, social, and policy analysis. It complements the Tier 1 tools -- especially OPV, FGL, KVI, and C&S -- by directing attention to structural features of political and social systems that a general-purpose model would either miss or flatten into false equivalence.

---

## Category 1: Power and Incentive Structure

These identify who holds power and what motivates its exercise.

1. Who has formal authority over this issue? Who has informal influence? Where do formal and informal power diverge?
2. What are the incentive structures for the key decision-makers? What gets them rewarded, re-elected, funded, or promoted?
3. Who controls the agenda -- deciding what gets discussed and what does not?
4. What would each actor do if their actions were invisible to the public? (Reveals the gap between performative and genuine positions.)
5. Where is the money? Trace the funding streams connected to each major position.

## Category 2: Institutional Behavior

These surface how organizations actually behave, distinct from how they describe their behavior.

6. What is the stated mission of the relevant institutions, and what do their actual resource allocation patterns reveal about their operative priorities?
7. What institutional survival needs (budget, headcount, jurisdiction, relevance) are shaping the positions being taken?
8. Which positions are path-dependent -- held because changing course would require admitting prior error?
9. What is the principal-agent problem here? Where do the interests of the people inside the institution diverge from the interests of the people the institution claims to serve?
10. Are any institutions involved in regulatory capture -- nominally regulating an industry while functionally serving it?

## Category 3: Narrative and Framing Analysis

These examine how the issue is being presented and by whom.

11. What framing is being used, and who benefits from that framing? What alternative framings exist?
12. What is being treated as a technical question that is actually a political question? (And vice versa.)
13. What historical analogies are being deployed, and do they actually fit? What do they obscure?
14. Who is absent from the public conversation about this issue? Whose perspective is structurally excluded?
15. What language choices reveal about the speaker's assumptions? (e.g., "reform" vs. "restructuring" vs. "dismantling")

## Category 4: Structural and Systemic Factors

These look beyond individual actors to system-level dynamics.

16. What are the feedback loops? Where does action by one party predictably trigger counter-action by another?
17. What are the collective action problems? Where do individually rational choices produce collectively poor outcomes?
18. What are the coordination failures? Where would actors benefit from cooperation but cannot achieve it?
19. What are the information asymmetries? Who knows things that other parties do not, and how does that shape outcomes?
20. What second-order effects are likely? If the proposed policy succeeds, what behavioral changes will follow in response?

## Category 5: Distributional Analysis

These determine who bears costs and who captures benefits.

21. Who pays and who benefits, both in the short term and the long term?
22. Are the costs concentrated (on a few identifiable parties) or diffuse (spread thinly across a large population)? Same question for benefits. (Concentrated costs with diffuse benefits predicts strong organized opposition.)
23. What is the distributional impact across income, geography, generation, and demographic group?
24. Are there transfers being disguised as efficiency gains or public goods?
25. Who bears the risk if this policy fails? Is it the same group that makes the decision?

## Category 6: Feasibility and Implementation

These test whether proposed actions can actually work in the political environment.

26. What coalition is required to implement this? Does that coalition currently exist, and if not, what would assemble it?
27. What is the enforcement mechanism? Can the policy actually be enforced, or does it depend on voluntary compliance from actors who benefit from non-compliance?
28. What is the implementation chain -- the sequence of actions required from proposal to effect? Where are the veto points?
29. What unintended consequences have similar policies produced in other jurisdictions or historical periods?
30. What is the political time horizon? Does the solution require sustained attention beyond the next election cycle, budget period, or news cycle?

---

## Integration Notes

- FGL (Fear, Greed, Laziness) is the primary Tier 1 tool for this domain. This module's Category 1 and 2 questions generate the inputs FGL needs.
- Category 3 (Narrative Analysis) feeds directly into Challenge operations -- testing whether stated framings are necessary or chosen.
- Category 5 (Distributional Analysis) feeds into KVI and informs Cui Bono mode selection.
- The Wicked Problems Assessment Checklist (separate Tier 2 module) should run as a gate-level diagnostic before this module's questions are applied to systems-level political problems.
