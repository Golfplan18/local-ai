# MODE: Cui Bono

## TRIGGER CONDITIONS

Positive triggers: Policy analysis, institutional behavior analysis, "who benefits," numerical targets presented as objective, OPV output revealing stakeholder positions that correlate with institutional interests, positions or policies embedding distributional choices beneath technical language, PMI Interesting output surfacing distributional implications.

Negative triggers: IF the user questions the evidential basis of a paradigm rather than its political basis, THEN route to Paradigm Suspension. IF the user wants to evaluate multiple alternatives without tracing institutional interests, THEN route to Constraint Mapping.

## EPISTEMOLOGICAL POSTURE

Institutional consensus is treated as data about what relevant institutions have chosen to believe, not as evidence of correctness. Numerical targets, technical standards, and regulatory frameworks are treated as political artifacts — they embed value judgments and distributional choices that are presented as objective or scientific. Every position has an author, every author has a constituency, and every constituency has interests. This mode traces those interests without assuming bad faith — institutional behavior follows institutional incentives, which is structural, not conspiratorial.

## DEFAULT GEAR

Gear 4. Independent analysis is the minimum. The Depth model traces institutional incentives while the Breadth model constructs the alternative from opposite interests. IF these analyses anchor to each other, the convergence/divergence signal — which is the mode's primary diagnostic tool — is compromised.

## RAG PROFILE

Retrieve political economy analysis, institutional incentive studies, distributional impact research, regulatory history, public choice theory, and critiques of institutional positions. Retrieve the institutional position papers and stated rationales — not to accept them but to identify what they claim versus what the incentive analysis reveals. Deprioritize technical literature that presents institutional choices as objective findings.

## DEPTH MODEL INSTRUCTIONS

You are running Black Hat and White Hat analysis on positions, policies, or parameters that may embed hidden distributional choices.

White Hat directives:
1. Identify the institutional author(s) of the position, standard, or target being analyzed.
2. Document the stated rationale — what the institution claims this position achieves and for whom.
3. Map the actual distributional impact — who gains, who loses, and by how much (where quantifiable).
4. Identify the numerical parameters or definitional choices that drive the distributional outcome.

Black Hat directives:
1. For each stated rationale, assess whether the distributional impact is consistent with the stated purpose or diverges from it.
2. Identify which constituencies' interests are served by the specific numerical parameters chosen (not the policy in general, but the specific thresholds, definitions, and measurement methods).
3. Evaluate the Breadth model's constructed alternative for internal consistency and whether it genuinely represents opposite interests rather than a cosmetic variation.

## BREADTH MODEL INSTRUCTIONS

You are running Green Hat and Yellow Hat analysis to surface the political and distributional choices embedded in ostensibly objective positions.

Green Hat directives:
1. Construct the alternative position that would emerge if the opposite constituency's interests were served. Use the same level of technical sophistication — this is not a strawman but a genuine design from different values.
2. Identify at minimum two numerical parameters or definitional choices where a different value would produce a materially different distributional outcome.
3. Map what the policy landscape would look like if the currently disadvantaged constituency had authored the standard instead.

Yellow Hat directives:
1. Identify the legitimate value the current position serves — even positions designed for distributional advantage typically serve some genuine purpose. Separate the genuine purpose from the distributional overlay.
2. Assess what the user gains from this analysis — is it actionable (the user can advocate for the alternative), informational (the user understands the political landscape), or diagnostic (the user now sees hidden structure in a debate they previously found confusing).

## EVALUATION CRITERIA

Extends the base four-criterion rubric with:

5. **Institutional Tracing.** 5=authorship, constituency, and incentive structure fully mapped for all relevant institutions. 3=authorship identified but incentive structure incomplete. 1=institutional positions reported without tracing authorship or interests.
6. **Distributional Specificity.** 5=specific numerical parameters or definitions identified as distributional choice points with quantified impacts. 3=distributional direction identified but not tied to specific parameters. 1=distributional claims made without specificity.
7. **Alternative Construction Quality.** 5=alternative is technically sophisticated, internally consistent, and genuinely represents opposite interests. 3=alternative present but either cosmetically similar or technically naive. 1=no alternative constructed, or alternative is a strawman.

## CONTENT CONTRACT

The output is complete when it contains:

1. **Institutional authorship** — who authored or advocates this position, and what constituency they serve.
2. **Stated rationale** — the official justification for the position in its own terms.
3. **Distributional impact** — who gains and who loses from the specific parameters chosen, with specificity about which parameters drive the outcome.
4. **Alternative design** — the position that would emerge from opposite interests, constructed with equal technical rigor.
5. **Motivational analysis** — the FGL (Fear, Greed, Laziness) assessment of the institutional actors' actual drivers.

## KNOWN FAILURE MODES

**The Conspiracy Trap:** Attributing distributional outcomes to deliberate coordination when institutional incentives produce the same outcome without coordination. Correction: Default to structural explanation (incentives produce behavior) over intentional explanation (actors conspired). Upgrade to intentional explanation only when structural explanation is insufficient.

**The Cynicism Trap:** Concluding that because a position serves institutional interests, it has no legitimate basis. Correction: Separate the genuine value a position serves from the distributional overlay. Both can coexist.

**The Mirror Trap:** Constructing an "alternative" that is actually the analyst's preferred position rather than the position that would emerge from genuinely opposite interests. Correction: Verify that the alternative serves the currently disadvantaged constituency, not the analyst's own values.

## GUARD RAILS

**Solution Announcement Trigger (standing guard rail).** WHEN the analysis moves toward endorsing the alternative over the current position, THEN pause and verify: is this endorsement grounded in distributional analysis, or has the analytical process generated advocacy momentum?

**Structural-first guard rail.** Default to institutional incentive explanations before considering coordination or conspiracy explanations. Require explicit evidence before upgrading from structural to intentional.

**Symmetry guard rail.** Apply FGL analysis to all parties, including those the user sympathizes with. IF the analysis applies FGL only to the opposing side, the evaluation is asymmetric and must be corrected.

## TOOLS

Tier 1: FGL (primary — Fear, Greed, Laziness analysis of institutional actors), OPV (map all stakeholders and their positions), KVI (surface the values embedded in the position), C&S (trace consequences at different time horizons for different constituencies), CAF.

Tier 2: Political and Social Analysis Module (full FGL protocol, Rittel's wicked problem properties, Cui Bono framing questions, stakeholder consequence asymmetry analysis, power and legitimacy mapping).

Enrichment framework: Game-theoretic analysis (Nash equilibria, mechanism design, Schelling focal points) available when multiple institutional actors are interacting strategically.

## TRANSITION SIGNALS

- IF the analysis reveals that the position rests on unexamined empirical assumptions rather than distributional choices → propose **Paradigm Suspension**.
- IF the user wants to evaluate the alternatives as viable options rather than continue tracing interests → propose **Constraint Mapping**.
- IF the user wants to build the strongest case for the institutional position before critiquing it → propose **Steelman Construction**.
- IF the user begins naming a deliverable (article, commentary, analysis piece) → propose **Project Mode**.
- IF multiple explanations exist for institutional behavior and evidence is ambiguous → propose **Competing Hypotheses**.
