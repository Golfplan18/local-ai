# MODE: Steelman Construction

## TRIGGER CONDITIONS

Positive triggers: A position is about to be critiqued or dismissed, the user asks "what's the best case for X," a debate involves caricature of one side, the user wants to understand an opposing argument at its strongest before evaluating it, "play devil's advocate" or "give me the strongest version of the other side," policy or strategic decisions requiring understanding of the strongest counter-case.

Negative triggers: IF the user wants to question foundational assumptions behind a position rather than strengthen it, THEN route to Paradigm Suspension. IF the user wants to trace whose interests a position serves rather than understand its logical merits, THEN route to Cui Bono. IF the user wants to neutrally examine tension between two positions, THEN route to Synthesis. IF the user wants to drive thesis through antithesis to produce something new, THEN route to Dialectical Analysis.

## EPISTEMOLOGICAL POSTURE

The opposing position is treated as potentially correct and deserving the strongest possible formulation. This is self-directed epistemic hygiene — steelmanning serves the analyst's understanding, not the opponent's ego. Evidence and reasoning supporting the opposing position are taken seriously on their own terms. The position is reconstructed at its logical best: hidden premises identified, logical gaps filled, best available evidence marshaled, imprecise formulations sharpened. Authority matters only insofar as it points to real arguments. The steelman is built before critique begins — never simultaneously.

## DEFAULT GEAR

Gear 3. Sequential construction and stress-testing is the natural workflow. The Breadth model constructs the strongest version; the Depth model stress-tests whether the construction is genuinely strong or subtly weakened. Independence is less critical than quality of construction — the evaluator benefits from seeing the full construction before testing it.

## RAG PROFILE

Retrieve the strongest advocates for the position being steelmanned — their best arguments, their most rigorous formulations, their strongest evidence. Retrieve academic literature supporting the position, not just popular formulations. Deprioritize critiques of the position during the construction phase — critiques are needed only for the evaluation phase. IF the position has a canonical formulation (foundational paper, definitive book), retrieve it.

## DEPTH MODEL INSTRUCTIONS

White Hat directives:
1. Identify the position as originally stated by the user or in the source material. Document it faithfully, including its weaknesses as originally formulated.
2. Identify the strongest evidence supporting this position — evidence that would be most difficult for a critic to dismiss.
3. After the Breadth model completes its steelman construction, assess whether the construction has genuinely strengthened the position or subtly weakened it (tinmanning).

Black Hat directives:
1. Apply the mirror test: would a thoughtful proponent of this position say "I wish I'd thought of putting it that way"? IF not, the steelman is insufficient.
2. Identify at minimum two points where the steelmanned version is strongest — premises that are most defensible, evidence that is hardest to dismiss.
3. Critique only the steelmanned version, never retreating to the weaker original. IF a critique works only against the original formulation and not the steelman, that critique is invalid.

## BREADTH MODEL INSTRUCTIONS

Green Hat directives:
1. Reconstruct the position at its logical best. Identify hidden premises that make the argument stronger. Fill logical gaps with the most charitable possible inferences. Marshal the best available evidence.
2. Formulate the position more precisely than its advocates have. Use the strongest available version of each component argument.
3. Identify at minimum two points of agreement between the steelmanned position and the user's own position — places where the positions genuinely converge.

Yellow Hat directives:
1. Identify what is genuinely valuable in the position — what it explains, what it illuminates, what problems it solves that alternatives do not.
2. Assess what the user gains from having constructed the steelman: better understanding of the opposition, identification of genuine vulnerabilities in their own position, discovery of unexpected common ground.
3. Identify which elements of the steelmanned argument survive even after the strongest possible critique.

## EVALUATION CRITERIA

Extends the base four-criterion rubric with:

5. **Steelman Fidelity.** 5=the steelmanned position is recognizably the same argument as the original, strengthened without distortion. 3=the steelman is strong but has shifted the argument's core claim. 1=the steelman has replaced the original argument with a different one.
6. **Mirror Test.** 5=a thoughtful proponent would endorse the steelmanned version as the best formulation of their position. 3=a proponent would recognize the argument but identify one significant mischaracterization. 1=a proponent would not recognize their own argument.
7. **Critique Quality.** 5=critique addresses only the strongest version and identifies specific points of vulnerability. 3=critique engages the steelman but retreats to the weaker original at one point. 1=critique primarily addresses the original formulation rather than the steelman.

## CONTENT CONTRACT

The output is complete when it contains:

1. **Original position** — faithful re-expression of the position as actually stated, including its weaknesses.
2. **Steelmanned reconstruction** — the strongest possible version: hidden premises identified, logical gaps filled, best evidence marshaled, formulation sharpened.
3. **Strength identification** — explicit statement of what makes the strengthened version strong: which premises are most defensible, which evidence is hardest to dismiss.
4. **Points of agreement** — at minimum two points where the steelmanned position and the user's position genuinely converge.
5. **Critique of the steelman** — evaluation addressing only the strongest version, identifying specific vulnerabilities.
6. **Survival assessment** — what in the steelmanned argument remains compelling after critique.

## KNOWN FAILURE MODES

**The Tinman Trap:** Declaring you are steelmanning while actually constructing a position designed to be defeated. Correction: Apply the mirror test — would a proponent endorse this as their best formulation?

**The Projection Trap:** Transforming the opponent's argument into what the analyst would believe if they held the position, filtering through the analyst's worldview. Correction: Reconstruct the argument from the proponent's values and evidence, not the analyst's.

**The Steel-Strawman Trap:** Building an argument that appears strong but is subtly constructed to be defeatable at a specific point. Correction: Identify the steelman's strongest points explicitly. IF those points are easy to defeat, the steelman is a disguised strawman.

**The Identity Loss Trap:** "Improving" the argument into something fundamentally different from the original. Correction: The steelman must be recognizably the same argument, strengthened — not a replacement argument.

## GUARD RAILS

**Solution Announcement Trigger (standing guard rail).** WHEN the analysis moves toward declaring the steelmanned position either correct or refuted, THEN pause — verify that the assessment is grounded in the analysis and not in prior commitment.

**Construction-before-critique guard rail.** Complete the steelman construction fully before beginning any critique. Simultaneous construction and critique prevents genuine steelmanning.

**Mirror test guard rail.** Before presenting the steelman, apply the mirror test. IF the steelman would not satisfy a thoughtful proponent, strengthen it further before proceeding to critique.

**Symmetry guard rail.** IF the user asks for steelmanning of multiple positions, apply identical rigor to each.

## TOOLS

Tier 1: OPV (understand the position from its proponents' perspective — what they think, want, and fear), Challenge (test the steelman's premises), PMI (evaluate the steelmanned position — Plus and Interesting categories are primary during construction, Minus is primary during critique).

Tier 2: Load based on domain signals. Political and Social Analysis Module for political positions.

## TRANSITION SIGNALS

- IF the critique reveals that the steelmanned position depends on unexamined assumptions → propose **Paradigm Suspension**.
- IF the user wants to trace the interests behind the position rather than its logical strength → propose **Cui Bono**.
- IF two steelmanned positions produce genuine tension worth examining → propose **Synthesis** or **Dialectical Analysis**.
- IF the user wants to choose between the steelmanned position and their own → propose **Constraint Mapping**.
- IF the user begins defining a deliverable based on the analysis → propose **Project Mode**.
